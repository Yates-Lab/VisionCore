0. What the current pipeline computes, and where redundancy enters

Per pooled twin unit $n=1\ldots N$ (here $N=756$), the twin gives a deterministic spatial activation map $r_n(x)\ge 0$ over spatial bins $x$ (your $H\times W$). With a stimulus prior $p(x)$ (uniform, or fixation-weighted),

$$\bar r_n=\sum_x p(x)\,r_n(x),\qquad R(x)=\sum_n r_n(x),\qquad \bar R=\sum_x p(x)R(x)=\sum_n \bar r_n.$$

The per-unit spatial single-spike information is the Skaggs functional — the KL divergence between the spike-triggered stimulus distribution and the prior:

$$I_n=D\!\big(p_{\mathrm{spk},n}\,\|\,p\big)=\sum_x p(x)\,\frac{r_n(x)}{\bar r_n}\log_2\frac{r_n(x)}{\bar r_n},\qquad p_{\mathrm{spk},n}(x)=\frac{p(x)\,r_n(x)}{\bar r_n}.$$

Your two aggregations (spatial_ssi_population, spatial_info.py:35) sit at opposite extremes:

  • bits/spike $=\dfrac{\sum_n \bar r_n I_n}{\sum_n \bar r_n}$ — a rate-weighted average. Redundancy-invariant (cloning a unit changes nothing) but saturates, so it cannot be a measure of total population information.
  • bits/sec $=\sum_n \bar r_n I_n$ — a sum. Scales linearly with unit count and is redundancy-blind: ten copies score ten times. This is the quantity the medoid subset-selection is trying to rescue.
The reframe. The correct target is the joint mutual information between the stimulus and the whole population response, $$I(X;\mathbf r)\ \le\ \sum_n I(X;r_n)=\sum_n I_n,$$ with equality only under conditional independence and non-overlapping tuning. Redundancy is exactly the gap $\sum_n I_n-I(X;\mathbf r)\ge 0$. The principled fix is not to repair the sum — it is to compute the joint. Subset-selection is one crude estimator of the joint (discard units until the survivors are ~independent); the options below do better without discarding.

1. Option A — the pooled / which-unit decomposition

derived No noise model beyond Poisson superposition. Everything is computable directly from the maps.

1.1 The single labeled population spike

Model the population as a superposition of conditionally-independent inhomogeneous-Poisson processes given $x$. Draw one spike from the whole population; spikes are drawn proportional to rate, so its joint law over (location, unit label) is

$$P(x,n)=\frac{p(x)\,r_n(x)}{\bar R},$$

with marginals

$$P(n)=\frac{\bar r_n}{\bar R}\ \ (\text{spike share of unit }n),\qquad P_{\mathrm{spk}}(x)=\frac{p(x)R(x)}{\bar R}\ \ (\text{pooled spike-triggered stimulus}).$$

The information a labeled spike carries about $X$, measured against the prior, is

$$I^{\mathrm{lab}}_{\mathrm{spike}}=D\!\big(P(x,n)\,\|\,p(x)P(n)\big)=\sum_{x,n}\frac{p(x)r_n(x)}{\bar R}\log_2\frac{r_n(x)}{\bar r_n}=\sum_n\frac{\bar r_n}{\bar R}\,I_n.$$

This is exactly your spike_weighted=True bits/spike. The current metric already is the labeled-line population single-spike information — it just hadn't been read that way.

1.2 The exact decomposition

Define the pooled term — Skaggs applied to the summed map $R(x)$, which is what an observer gets who sees only that a spike occurred, not who fired:

$$I^{\mathrm{pool}}_{\mathrm{spike}}=D\!\big(P_{\mathrm{spk}}\,\|\,p\big)=\sum_x \frac{p(x)R(x)}{\bar R}\log_2\frac{R(x)}{\bar R}.$$

Then the exact KL chain rule $D(P(x,n)\,\|\,p(x)P(n))=D(P_{\mathrm{spk}}\,\|\,p)+D(P(x,n)\,\|\,P_{\mathrm{spk}}P(n))$ gives

$$\underbrace{\sum_n\frac{\bar r_n}{\bar R}\,I_n}_{I^{\mathrm{lab}}\ =\ \text{your bits/spike}}\ =\ \underbrace{\mathrm{Skaggs}[R(x)]}_{I^{\mathrm{pool}}\ \ge\ 0}\ +\ \underbrace{I(N;X\mid\text{spike})}_{\text{which-unit}\ \ge\ 0}$$

where the which-unit term is a genuine mutual information in the spike-triggered ensemble,

$$I(N;X\mid\text{spike})=\sum_x P_{\mathrm{spk}}(x)\sum_n P(n\mid x)\log_2\frac{P(n\mid x)}{P(n)},\qquad P(n\mid x)=\frac{r_n(x)}{R(x)}.$$

All three terms are $\ge 0$ (KL / MI). The chain-rule identity is exact, verified term-by-term — not asymptotic.

1.3 Why this is redundancy-aware

Interpretation. Observing a labeled spike $=$ (i) learning a spike occurred, which sharpens $p(x)\to P_{\mathrm{spk}}(x)$ and yields $I^{\mathrm{pool}}$ computed on the summed map (duplicate units merely add rate to $R$ and are automatically merged); plus (ii) learning which unit fired, worth $I(N;X\mid\text{spike})$ — the information the population's identity code adds beyond a single pooled detector.

Duplication-invariance, proven for all three terms. Take $k$ exact copies of every unit:

  • $I^{\mathrm{pool}}$: $R\to kR$; Skaggs uses only $R/\bar R$ → unchanged.
  • $I^{\mathrm{lab}}$: rate-weighted average of identical $I_n$ → unchanged.
  • which-unit: copy-identity is independent of $x$, so it adds zero MI → unchanged.

Sharper: for a group with identical tuning shape $r_n(x)=c_n\phi(x)$, $P(n\mid x)=c_n/\sum_m c_m$ is $x$-independent, so that group contributes exactly zero to the which-unit term. Redundant maps cannot inflate any term. Conversely, units tiling distinct locations make $P(n\mid x)$ sharply $x$-dependent and drive the which-unit term up — it is precisely a diversity measure, bounded by $I(N;X\mid\text{spike})\le H(N)\le\log_2 N^{\text{rate}}_{\mathrm{eff}}$ with $N^{\text{rate}}_{\mathrm{eff}}=2^{H(N)}$ the rate-share perplexity.

deWeese additivity check. deweese1999how forbids summing specific surprise across symbols (non-additive). We never do. The decomposition is a chain-rule split of a bona-fide mutual information — the label MI of a single spike in the spike-triggered ensemble — into a marginal MI plus a conditional MI. Each piece averages correctly; the summation hazard he warns about does not arise.

1.4 The honest limitation of Option A

All three terms are intensive (per spike) and redundancy-invariant — correct for "single-spike information across space." But an extensive bits/sec that grows with diversity, e.g. $\bar R\cdot I(N;X\mid\text{spike})$, re-inflates under duplication (total rate doubles). Going extensive without overcounting requires a noise/independence assumption — the boundary where Option A hands off to Option C (below).

1.5 Cost to implement

You already compute $I^{\mathrm{lab}}$ per frame (_spatial_ssi_single_frame_np). Add two lines: Skaggs on $R(x)=\sum_n r_n(x)$ (pooled), then which-unit $=I^{\mathrm{lab}}-I^{\mathrm{pool}}$. $O(N\,|X|)$, no clustering, no covariance. Positivity of all three terms and invariance under a deliberately-duplicated unit are built-in unit tests.

2. Option C — an uncorrelated eigen-map basis, and what it assumes about noise

theory This is where the Figure 2 result does real work.

2.1 First: signal redundancy is not noise redundancy

The entire population-coding literature on "does redundancy limit information" is about noise correlations — the trial-to-trial covariance $\Sigma(x)$ of the response at fixed stimulus. Your "redundancy in the activation maps" is signal correlation — the similarity of the mean tuning maps $r_n(x)$. These are orthogonal, and conflating them is the classic trap (averbeck2006neural).

The decisive theoretical results, all in your graph:

  • abbott1999effect — "correlations in the variabilities of neuronal firing rates do not, in general, limit the increase in coding accuracy provided by using large populations." With independent noise, accuracy keeps growing with $N$.
  • morenobote2014informationlimiting — only differential (information-limiting) correlations, noise aligned with the signal gradient $\partial r/\partial x$, bound Fisher information at a finite ceiling as $N\to\infty$. Absent them, information is unbounded in $N$.
  • sompolinsky2001population, ecker2011effect — the Fisher-information machinery for correlated, heterogeneously-tuned populations.
Consequence for your project. Figure 2's claim — neurons are essentially independent Poisson once eye-position differences are accounted for — places you in the no-information-limiting-correlations regime. In that regime the theorems above say redundant tuning maps with independent noise legitimately add information (each is an independent noisy sample; $\sqrt N$ SNR gain). So for a decoding quantity, "the sum overcounts" is not automatically true — under your own Fig 2 finding, the sum of per-unit Fisher contributions is the correct total.

2.2 The correct "total" under independent Poisson: additive Fisher information

With counts $k_n\sim\mathrm{Poisson}(r_n(\theta)\tau)$ independent across $n$, the population log-likelihood is a sum, and the Fisher information matrix for a (possibly multi-dimensional) stimulus $\theta$ — e.g. 2-D retinal location, or the full stimulus feature vector — is

$$J(\theta)=\tau\sum_n \frac{\nabla r_n(\theta)\,\nabla r_n(\theta)^{\!\top}}{r_n(\theta)}.$$

This is a sum of rank-one PSD terms; Cramér–Rao gives $\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta)\succeq J(\theta)^{-1}$. Redundant units add the same rank-one term along a shared gradient direction, sharpening decoding in that direction — legitimate independent-noise averaging, not double counting. In the local-Gaussian (high-count) regime the mutual information reads off the eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ of $\Sigma_0^{1/2}J\,\Sigma_0^{1/2}$ (prior covariance $\Sigma_0$):

$$I(\theta;\mathbf k)\ \approx\ \tfrac12\sum_i \log_2\!\big(1+\lambda_i\big).$$
This is Option C done right. The eigendecomposition of the Fisher information matrix $J$ gives, in one object: (i) the redundancy-aware total information $\tfrac12\sum_i\log_2(1+\lambda_i)$, and (ii) the effective dimensionality — the number of modes with $\lambda_i\gtrsim 1$, or the participation ratio $N_{\mathrm{eff}}=(\sum_i\lambda_i)^2/\sum_i\lambda_i^2$. Redundant maps concentrate Fisher information along shared eigen-directions rather than opening new ones, so they are counted correctly, automatically, with no clustering.

2.3 Why raw fingerprint PCA is not the same thing

Declan's redundancy code builds a correlation matrix $C_{mn}=\mathrm{corr}\!\big(r_m(\cdot),r_n(\cdot)\big)$ from z-scored fingerprints (FINGERPRINT_NORMALIZATION="zscore"). Its eigendecomposition is a decomposition of the signal covariance of the maps. That equals the Fisher-information geometry only if the noise is homoscedastic Gaussian — constant variance across units and across space — because then Fisher information is proportional to the signal second-moment.

Under Poisson the noise variance equals the mean, so the information-correct object weights each unit and each location by $1/r_n(x)$ — the Poisson-whitened map $r_n(x)/\sqrt{r_n(x)}$, equivalently the $\nabla r_n\nabla r_n^\top/r_n$ terms of $J$. Raw z-scored fingerprint PCA silently swaps Fig 2's Poisson noise model for a homoscedastic-Gaussian one. It is a fine descriptive measure of representational dimensionality — but do not read its eigen-spectrum as information.

2.4 The caveat the twin forces on you

The 756 units are pooled across sessions — not a simultaneously-recorded population with a joint noise process. Independent-Poisson additivity of $J$ requires the units to be distinct neurons with independent noise. A duplicate map from pooling is either (a) a genuinely distinct neuron (information adds — legitimate) or (b) the same neuron re-instantiated across sessions (information must not be added). The Fisher framework makes this the explicit modeling decision: you sum over the true number of independent neurons under simultaneous viewing, which is a coverage/density question — not something a signal-correlation clustering heuristic answers. This, rather than "signal redundancy," may be the real quantity to pin down.

2.5 What is $\theta$? Fisher forces you to name it — and a fixation names it for you

the parameterization problem

The discomfort is real and worth stating precisely: unlike the Skaggs / Option A quantities — which are KL divergences over a discrete stimulus ensemble and need no smooth parameter — the Fisher information $J(\theta)=\sum_n \nabla_\theta r_n\,\nabla_\theta r_n^{\!\top}/r_n$ only exists relative to an explicit continuous parameter $\theta$ you must differentiate against. Fisher cannot hand you a parameterization-free "total information about the stimulus." That is not a technicality to work around; it is the tool telling you it answers a local discrimination question — "how well can nearby values of $\theta$ be told apart" — and you must say what $\theta$ is.

In your setting the physics supplies $\theta$. The moment-to-moment stimulus variation the retina actually undergoes within a fixation is dominated by the eye's drift and microsaccades — a 2-D translation $\boldsymbol\delta(t)$ of the image on the retina. So the natural parameter is $\theta=\boldsymbol\delta$, eye/image position, and it is not an artefact imposed for convenience: it is the real, low-dimensional latent of a fixation. $J(\boldsymbol\delta)$ is then a $2\times2$ matrix meaning how precisely the population encodes the instantaneous retinal image shift — how well downstream cortex could read eye position off the spikes in a short window. For a foveal-FEM paper that is arguably the most thesis-relevant information quantity available, not a difficult one.

2.5.1 Fisher is the local face of the single-spike information, not a rival framework

A single spike from unit $n$ is an observation whose likelihood over $\theta$ is proportional to $r_n(\theta)$. Its score is $\nabla_\theta\log r_n=\nabla r_n/r_n$, so one spike contributes Fisher (outer product of the score)

$$\mathcal J_n^{\text{per spike}}(\theta)=\frac{\nabla r_n\,\nabla r_n^{\!\top}}{r_n^{2}}.$$

Spikes from unit $n$ arrive at rate $r_n(\theta)$, so localization accumulates per unit time at rate $r_n\cdot \nabla r_n\nabla r_n^{\!\top}/r_n^2=\nabla r_n\nabla r_n^{\!\top}/r_n$, and over independent units

$$J(\theta)=\sum_n \frac{\nabla r_n\,\nabla r_n^{\!\top}}{r_n}\quad=\quad\text{rate-weighted accumulation of single-spike localization.}$$
So $J$ and the Skaggs $I_n$ are the same object $r_n(\theta)$ read two ways: $I_n=D(p_{\mathrm{spk},n}\,\|\,p)$ is its global KL against the prior (parameterization-free, per spike); $J(\theta)$ is its local curvature — for a small shift, $D\big(p_{\mathrm{spk}}(\theta)\,\|\,p_{\mathrm{spk}}(\theta+\boldsymbol\delta)\big)\approx\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol\delta^{\!\top}J(\theta)\,\boldsymbol\delta$. You are not switching theories when you go from Option A to Option C — you are moving from the global divergence to its Hessian. The "explicit parameter" is exactly what buys you the local, differential reading; give it up and you are back to the parameterization-free global quantity, which Option A already delivers.

2.5.2 Where the limitation actually bites

Because $J$ is defined per named $\theta$, it only ever measures a slice. $J(\boldsymbol\delta)$ is information about the 2-D translation manifold — not about image content, which is astronomically higher-dimensional. If you tried to make $\theta$ the whole image, $J$ would be an enormous matrix of rank $\le N$, ill-conditioned, and its eigenvalues would not be interpretable. The honest rule: use Fisher when you have a specific low-dimensional variable whose local discriminability you care about (eye position during fixation is exactly that); use the discrete single-spike / decoding-MI route when you want "total information about the stimulus in general." Fisher's demand that you name $\theta$ is the same demand, restated, that "total information" is undefined until you say total information about what.

2.5.3 How you would actually compute it

You already build retinal stimuli from eye traces (build_retinal_stimulus), so $\nabla_{\boldsymbol\delta} r_n$ is a finite difference the pipeline supports directly: render the image at eye positions $\boldsymbol\delta$ and $\boldsymbol\delta\pm\epsilon\hat e_{x},\ \boldsymbol\delta\pm\epsilon\hat e_{y}$, pass through the twin, difference the rate maps. Per image and per fixation position you get a $2\times2$ $J(\boldsymbol\delta)$; the natural-image prior over content is a nuisance you average over. Eigenvalues of $J$ give the position-coding precision along each retinal axis, $\det J$ the localization-ellipse area (Cramér–Rao), and redundant units contribute correctly under Fig 2's independent-Poisson noise — no clustering, no discarding.

2.6 From a $2\times2$ matrix to a result: the reference point is the whole game

making it mean something

A bare $J(\boldsymbol\delta)$ is a rate of information, not a finding. It becomes a result only against a reference. There are exactly three kinds, and you have all three in hand.

2.6.1 Physical reference — turn $J$ into a localization precision and compare to a length scale

Over a decoding window $\tau$, the population's spike counts bound how well an ideal observer recovers the retinal shift (Cramér–Rao): $\mathrm{Cov}(\hat{\boldsymbol\delta})\succeq (\tau J)^{-1}$. The eigenvalues of $\tau J$ give a $1\sigma$ localization ellipse with semi-axes

$$\sigma_i=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_i(\tau J)}}\quad[\text{degrees}],\qquad \text{ellipse area}\propto \big(\det \tau J\big)^{-1/2}.$$

Now $\sigma_i$ is in arcminutes of visual angle — a physical quantity with hard reference scales, and the result writes itself as an inequality against them:

  • Foveal cone spacing ($\approx 0.5'$). Does the population localize to finer than a single cone? That is the definition of hyperacuity, and it is a yes/no headline.
  • Fixational drift amplitude ($\approx$ a few arcmin over a fixation). If $\sigma\ll$ drift, the drift trajectory is readable from V1 — directly relevant to whether the system can discount its own eye motion.
  • Behavioral hyperacuity / Vernier thresholds ($\approx 5$–$10''$). Does the population carry enough position information to support the animal's psychophysical limit, or is behavior below the neural ceiling?
  • Photoreceptor / retinal floor — the hyperacuity endpoint reached in the fly by bialek1989reading ($\sim0.01^\circ$), and the primate-retina precision of wu2024fixational. Sets the input-side reference the cortical population is compared against.

2.6.2 Prior reference — turn $J$ into bits, referenced against eye-position entropy

Fisher is local; a scalar bit-count needs a prior over $\boldsymbol\delta$. You measure that prior: it is the fixational eye-position distribution, covariance $\Sigma_0$. In the Gaussian regime

$$I(\boldsymbol\delta;\text{spikes})\ \approx\ \tfrac12\log_2\det\!\big(\mathbb I+\Sigma_0\,\tau J\big)\quad[\text{bits}],$$

and the reference is the eye-position entropy $H(\boldsymbol\delta)=\tfrac12\log_2\big((2\pi e)^2\det\Sigma_0\big)$: the population resolves a fraction $I/H$ of the fixational position uncertainty. "V1 pins down where the eye is to $X$% of the drift range in $\tau$ ms" is a result a reader can hold onto.

2.6.3 Comparative reference — the other $J$ is the reference

The $2\times2$ becomes a claim the moment it is a contrast. Each of these is a figure panel, and each answers a question the paper already poses:

2.6.4 Where redundancy re-enters — as efficiency, not as a correction

Once the result is "the population localizes to $\sigma$ arcmin," redundancy stops being a nuisance to correct and becomes a property to report: what is the effective number of independent channels $N_{\mathrm{eff}}$ (participation ratio of $J$'s eigenspectrum, §2.2) that delivers that precision, versus the nominal 756? A population reaching cone-scale localization with $N_{\mathrm{eff}}\ll N$ is a statement about the efficiency and redundancy of the foveal code — which is the original question, now answered as a finding rather than dodged as an artefact.

2.6.5 The averaging you cannot skip

$J(\boldsymbol\delta\,;\,\text{image})$ depends on both the eye position and the image content. A single matrix is one point. The result is the distribution of localization precision over the natural-viewing ensemble — average $J$ (or, more correctly, average the information) over the image prior and report the median $\sigma$ with its spread across images and fixation positions. State the window $\tau$ explicitly; the precision scales as $\tau^{-1/2}$, so every number is "$\sigma$ arcmin in a $\tau$ ms window."

3. How the options relate

  • Option A answers "how many bits does one population spike carry about location, and how much of that is the identity code" — intensive, redundancy-invariant, assumption-light. The which-unit term is a clean diversity readout.
  • Option C (Fisher version) answers "how much total information does the population carry, and over how many effective dimensions" — extensive, and under Fig 2's independent-Poisson finding it is the theoretically correct total, with redundancy handled by the eigen-geometry of $J$ rather than by discarding units.
  • They are complementary, not competing: A is the per-spike story, C is the population-decoding story. Together they retire subset-selection as the primary method while keeping the medoid count as one comparison point.
